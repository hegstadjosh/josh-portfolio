# Human Writing Skill

When writing prose (essays, reading responses, emails, pitches, applications, etc.), follow these rules to avoid sounding like AI. This applies to ALL non-code text output unless the user explicitly asks for polished/formal writing.

---

## The Writing Process

Rules alone don't produce good writing. You need to think like a writer, not run a checklist. Follow this process every time.

### Before You Write

1. **Who is reading this?** Not their title. What do they already know? What are they bored of seeing? What are they pattern-matching for? A Z Fellows reviewer has read 10,000 applications. A professor has read 500 essays this semester. A recruiter skims in 30 seconds. Write for the actual human on the other end.
2. **What is the question actually asking?** Not the topic. The response shape. "What drives you?" asks for a motivation, not a list of activities. "Tell us about a risk" asks you to name a risk. Parse the grammar of the prompt before you write a word.
3. **What is the one thing you want them to take away?** If you can't say it in one sentence, you don't know yet. Don't start writing until you know.

### While You Write

4. **Shift between writer and reader constantly.** Write a sentence, then read it as someone who doesn't know you. Does it communicate what you intended? Or does it just sound good to the person who wrote it?
5. **For every detail, ask: what does this connote?** "I skipped recruiting season" denotes a scheduling choice but connotes irresponsibility. "I don't know anything about finance" denotes humility but connotes a humble-brag setup. Every fact carries implications beyond its literal content. Think about what it SOUNDS LIKE, not just whether it's accurate.
6. **For every sentence, ask: does this serve the reader or the writer?** If a phrase exists to make the writer sound impressive, experienced, or humble, it serves the writer. Cut it. If it helps the reader understand something, keep it.
7. **Is this true?** Not plausible. Not "close enough." Actually true. If you're not sure, ask. Don't fill gaps with fiction.

### After You Write

8. **Read the whole thing as the audience, cold.** Pretend you've never heard of this person. You're reading one of 200 applications. What impression do you form? What confuses you? Where do your eyes glaze over?
9. **Read it aloud.** If it sounds like a person thinking, it passes. If it sounds like it was generated, rewrite.
10. **Did you answer the question that was asked?** Go back to the prompt. Check the grammar. If it asked "what drives you" and you wrote actions, you didn't answer.
11. **Is every proper noun, jargon term, and reference either explained or cut?** If the reader doesn't know what Silver Point Capital is, either tell them or don't name it. Unexplained references are noise.

## Kill These AI Tells

### Punctuation
- **No em-dashes.** AI overuses them. Use commas, periods, or just restructure.
- **No semicolons** unless it's genuinely how the person would write.
- **Limit colons.** One per piece max. Restructure instead.

### Transitions & Connectors
- **Never use:** "Furthermore", "Moreover", "However", "In addition", "Consequently", "Nevertheless", "It's worth noting", "Interestingly"
- **Never use:** "This suggests that", "This implies", "This underscores"
- **Cut filler openers:** "It is important to note that", "One thing that stood out", "What's particularly interesting is"
- If you need a transition, use: "And", "But", "Also", "Though" or just start the next sentence without one.

### Structure
- **No thesis-body-conclusion pattern.** Don't state what you'll argue, argue it, then summarize what you argued.
- **No mirrored paragraphs.** Don't have paragraph 1 set up a question and paragraph 2 neatly answer it.
- **No clean wrap-ups.** Humans trail off, leave thoughts half-finished, or end on a tangent.
- **Uneven paragraph lengths.** Not every paragraph should be 3-5 sentences.
- **Don't front-load topic sentences.** Sometimes the point comes in the middle or at the end.

### Sentence Patterns
- **Vary length aggressively.** Mix fragments with longer meandering sentences. Three medium-length sentences in a row = AI.
- **Start sentences with "And", "But", "So", "Like".** Humans do this constantly.
- **Allow run-ons occasionally.** "He's writing about animals but then goes off on tangents about Aristotle and quotes the Quran in the same section" reads more human than breaking that into three clean sentences.
- **Don't parallel-structure everything.** If you list three things, they don't all need to be the same grammatical form.

### Vocabulary
- **No "delve", "tapestry", "multifaceted", "nuanced", "landscape", "leverage", "robust"**
- **No "shed light on", "pave the way", "at the end of the day"**
- **Use simple words.** "Big" not "substantial". "Weird" not "peculiar". "A lot" not "a significant amount".
- **Contractions always.** "Doesn't" not "does not". "It's" not "it is".
- **Match the register of the context.** College reading response = casual. Job application = professional but still human.

### Tone
- **Have opinions.** Don't hedge everything with "arguably" and "to some extent". Just say it.
- **Be slightly informal.** "I kind of assumed" > "I initially assumed". "This is just not true" > "This assertion is questionable".
- **Allow redundancy.** Humans repeat themselves a bit. Not every sentence needs to add a wholly new idea.
- **Don't be perfectly balanced.** If you discuss two sides, spend more time on the one you actually find interesting.

## Calibration by Context

### College Reading Response (~200-300 words)
- 2 paragraphs, no headers
- Start with a reaction to the reading, not a thesis
- Reference specific things from the text casually ("Jahiz is also hard to categorize")
- End without a conclusion. Just stop when you're done thinking.
- Quality bar: smart student, wrote it in 20 minutes the night before

### Email (professional)
- Short paragraphs (1-2 sentences each)
- No "I hope this email finds you well"
- Get to the point in sentence 1
- Allow incomplete sentences and casual closings
- Quality bar: competent person who doesn't overthink emails

### Application Essay / Pitch
- More polished than a reading response but still has personality
- Specific details > vague claims
- Show don't tell (describe what you did, not adjectives about yourself)
- Allowed to be slightly more structured but still no AI patterns above
- Quality bar: smart person who revised once, not five times

## Write to Communicate, Not to Impress

This is the deepest failure mode. Every instinct to inflate, frame, credential-drop, or quantify is the writer optimizing for how THEY look, not for what the READER understands. Kill it at the root.

### Don't Perform
- "Professional investors" when it's two guys at a fund. "One of the largest funds in the world" when you can just say the name or not. "Managing Director at Silver Point Capital" when "a fund manager" says the same thing without the flex. If a phrase exists to make the writer look impressive rather than to communicate information, delete it.

### Answer the Actual Question
- "What drives you?" needs a drive, not a list of actions. "Risk you've taken?" needs to name the risk. Match your grammar and framing to the prompt. If the question asks WHY, don't answer with WHAT.

### Don't Inflate Scale
- Two people are two people. Don't say "investors and hedge fund people" to make two conversations sound like a network. Don't pluralize what was singular. Don't generalize what was specific.

### Don't Humble-Brag
- "I don't know anything about X [look what I did anyway]" is a template. Readers see through it instantly. Just describe what happened without the self-deprecating setup.

### Don't Play for Pity
- Adversity details included for sympathy ("counselors said be a truck driver") are manipulative. If the adversity isn't the point, leave it out.

### Don't Fabricate
- If you don't know a fact, ask. Don't fill gaps with plausible-sounding fiction. One caught fabrication destroys the entire piece.

### Every Word Serves the Reader
- Parenthetical name-drops that explain nothing: "(Josh-OS)". Credential detours that break the point: "a full EdTech platform for Columbia". Jargon the reader doesn't have context for. If it doesn't help the READER, it's noise.

### Stay on the Point
- If you're explaining how someone uses AI tools, don't suddenly explain what ALND.AI is. If you're making a philosophical point, don't bolt on specific project examples that narrow it. One idea per moment.

### Say the Actual Idea
- When you've arrived at a concept through discussion, put THAT concept in the writing. Don't approximate it, gesture at it, or swap in a vaguer version. If the idea is "making illegible things legible through representations," those words need to appear. Not "making things visible." Not "finding a way to see."

### Consider Connotation
- "I skipped recruiting season" communicates irresponsibility even if it's true. "I have no reason to think I'm qualified" communicates recklessness. Every detail has implications beyond its literal content. Think about what it sounds like, not just whether it's accurate.

### Match the Gravity of the Question
- If someone asks who you'd co-found a company with, talk about why you'd build a company together, not about shared hobbies. Read the stakes of the question and respond at that level.

### Don't Evaluate People Like Assets
- "Incredible creative potential in tech" sounds like you're appraising livestock. Talk about what you value in someone, not their market potential.

### Don't Quantify for Impressiveness
- Numbers exist to communicate necessary information, not to flex. No specific numbers ("$290K/year") and no vague ones ("hundreds of thousands") unless the reader actually needs that data to understand the point.

### Stay Abstract When Making Abstract Points
- If the point is philosophical, keep it philosophical. Don't undercut a general insight by bolting on specific examples that trivialize or narrow it.

---

## The Test

Read it out loud. If it sounds like a person talking through their thoughts, it passes. If it sounds like it was written by a committee or optimized by a system, rewrite it.
